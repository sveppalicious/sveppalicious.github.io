| **Palaeognathae** | Flightless ratites and tinamous share a distinctive primitive ("paleognathous") palate and other basal skull characters. The clade includes about 59 extant species in five orders (ratites like ostrich, emu, cassowary, rhea, kiwi plus ~46 tinamous). Most paleognaths are large, terrestrial birds and (except tinamous) lack a keeled sternum. | [Palaeognathae (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaeognathae)<br>[Palaeognathae (bird-phylogeny)](https://www.bird-phylogeny.de/superorders/palaeognathae/) |
| **Neognathae** | Neognaths are united by a "new" (neognathous) palate and other skeletal traits (e.g. fused hand bones). This infraclass contains ~10,000 living bird species - essentially all modern birds except the paleognaths. It splits into Galloanserae (fowl) and Neoaves. Neognaths exhibit vast diversity (e.g. passerines alone are ~60% of species) and occupy nearly every habitat. | [Neognathae (GBIF)](https://www.gbif.org/species/165842437)<br>[Neognathae (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neognathae) |
| **Galloanserae** | Galloanserae ("fowl") unite landfowl (Galliformes: chickens, pheasants, etc.) and waterfowl (Anseriformes: ducks, geese, swans). Together they number several hundred species (~307 galliforms + ~178 anseriforms). They share reproductive traits (large clutches of precocial young) and are considered the sister group to all other neognaths. | [Fowl / Galloanserae (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fowl)<br>[Galloanserae (bird-phylogeny)](https://www.bird-phylogeny.de/superorders/galloanserae/) |
| **Neoaves** | Neoaves comprises all modern birds outside Paleognathae and Galloanserae - roughly 95% of living species (~9,500). This huge clade underwent a rapid Cenozoic radiation and contains most bird orders. Because of its diversity, Neoaves has no single obvious anatomical synapomorphy; it is defined by genome-scale analyses. It includes diverse groups such as shorebirds, flamingos+grebes, nightbirds, core waterbirds, and core landbirds. | [Neoaves (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoaves)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
| **Mirandornithes** | Mirandornithes ("miracle birds") unites flamingos (order Phoenicopteriformes, ~6 species) and grebes (Podicipediformes, ~22 species). These ~28 species share unique skeletal synapomorphies (e.g. elongate neck vertebrae) despite differing ecologies (filter-feeding vs. diving). Genetic data and morphology support their sister relationship. | [Mirandornithes (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirandornithes)<br>[Sangster 2005, *A name for the flamingo-grebe clade*](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1474-919X.2005.00432.x) |
| **Columbaves** | Columbaves is a genomic clade uniting Columbimorphae (pigeons, sandgrouse, mesites) with Otidimorphae (bustards, cuckoos, turacos). It contains on the order of ~500-550 species (e.g. ~353 pigeons/doves, 16 sandgrouse, 3 mesites; ~156 cuckoos, 26 bustards, 23 turacos). No obvious unifying morphology is known beyond genetic support, but these are primarily terrestrial landbirds of the Old World. | [Columbaves (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbaves)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
| **Columbimorphae** | Columbimorphae unites pigeons/doves (Columbiformes), sandgrouse (Pterocliformes), and mesites (Mesitornithiformes). These birds are mostly ground-dwelling or arboreal seed-eaters/insectivores. The clade contains roughly 370 species (about 353 pigeons, 16 sandgrouse, 3 mesites). It was defined by molecular phylogenetics and has no single obvious external synapomorphy. | [Columbimorphae (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbimorphae)<br>[bird-phylogeny overview](https://www.bird-phylogeny.de/superorders/) |
| **Otidimorphae** | Otidimorphae groups bustards (Otidiformes), turacos (Musophagiformes), and cuckoos (Cuculiformes) into one clade. First supported by genome studies, it includes roughly 200 species (e.g. ~26 bustards, 23 turacos, ~156 cuckoos). Members vary widely (ground-dwelling herbivores like bustards, arboreal insectivores like cuckoos) and lack a clear shared morphology beyond DNA evidence. | [Otidimorphae (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otidimorphae)<br>[bird-phylogeny overview](https://www.bird-phylogeny.de/superorders/) |
| **Elementaves** | Elementaves is a newly defined clade comprising the hoatzin (Opisthocomiformes), cranes & rails (Gruiformes), and shorebirds (Charadriiformes). It is supported by recent genomic analyses, though no obvious morphological trait unites them. (One proposed synapomorphy is a pronounced ventral curvature of the scapula.) The clade contains on the order of ~500 species (mostly rails and shorebirds). | [Elementaves (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementaves)<br>[Pectoral girdle and forelimb skeleton paper (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12409627/) |
| **Cursorimorphae** | Cursorimorphae specifically refers to the clade of Gruiformes (cranes & rails) plus Charadriiformes (shorebirds). This sister-group relationship is repeatedly recovered in molecular studies. There is no obvious morphological synapomorphy beyond genetics. The clade encompasses several hundred species (mainly rails, cranes, and waders/shorebirds). | [Cursorimorphae / Gruimorphae (bird-phylogeny)](https://www.bird-phylogeny.de/superorders/cursorimorphae/)<br>[Pectoral girdle and forelimb skeleton paper (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12409627/) |
| **Strisores** | Strisores (Caprimulgimorphae) includes all nightbirds (Caprimulgiformes, Steatornithiformes, etc.) together with Apodiformes (swifts and hummingbirds). This group (~600 species) is united by genetic evidence and subtle skeletal traits (e.g. small hypocleideum on the furcula). Members often have wide gapes with rictal bristles (nightjars) or highly modified wings (swifts) reflecting their nocturnal or aerial specializations. | [Strisores (bird-phylogeny)](https://www.bird-phylogeny.de/superorders/strisores/)<br>[Strisores (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strisores) |
| **Phaethontimorphae** | Phaethontimorphae (a.k.a. Eurypygimorphae) links the tropical seabirds (tropicbirds, order Phaethontiformes, 3 species) with the Eurypygiformes (kagu and sunbittern, 2 species). This surprising clade was discovered by genomic analysis. It has only 5 species total and no obvious shared ecology or gross anatomy; the relationship rests on molecular data. | [Eurypygimorphae / Phaethontimorphae (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurypygimorphae)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
| **Aequinornithia** | Aequinornithia (core waterbirds) corresponds to the Aequornithes clade defined as the least crown group containing loons and pelicans. It includes loons, penguins, petrels (albatrosses & shearwaters), storks, pelicans, cormorants, and allies. Members share aquatic adaptations (e.g. dense bones, webbed feet). This group has ~340 species and is strongly supported by molecular studies. | [Aequornithes (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aequornithes)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
| **Telluraves** | Telluraves (core landbirds) is the clade uniting Australaves and Afroaves. It includes all passerines, parrots, falcons (Australaves) plus raptors, owls, woodpeckers, etc. (Afroaves). This enormous clade contains well over 7,000 species (passerines alone ~6,000). It is defined by genetics and characterized by an arboreal lifestyle rather than any single morphological trait. | [Telluraves (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telluraves)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
| **Afroaves** | Afroaves is the subgroup of Telluraves including predatory/insectivorous lineages: Coraciiformes (kingfishers), Piciformes (woodpeckers), Bucerotiformes (hornbills), Trogoniformes, Coliiformes (mousebirds), Strigiformes (owls), and Accipitriformes (hawks/eagles). These ~1,000+ species are united by DNA data. Notably, owls and hawks form a sister clade, suggesting the common ancestor of Afroaves may have been raptorial. | [Afroaves (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afroaves)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
| **Australaves** | Australaves is the clade of Cariamiformes (seriemas and extinct "terror birds") plus Eufalconimorphae (passerines, parrots, falcons). It includes ~6,000 songbirds, ~350 parrots, ~60 falcons, and 2 seriemas. The name reflects a Southern Hemisphere origin (passerines/parrots in Australia; falcons/seriemas in South America). Genome studies consistently recover this grouping. | [Australaves (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australaves)<br>[Stiller et al. 2024 (Nature)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07323-1) |
